# Super Bock Group -- Strategic Profile

## Company Overview -- #1 Beer in Portugal Since 1992, 47% Market Share, Carlsberg 60% Economic Interest

- **Full name**: Super Bock Group, SGPS, S.A. (formerly Unicer, renamed November 2017)
- **Founded**: 1890 (as CUFP - Companhia Uniao Fabril Portuense)
- **Headquarters**: Leca do Balio, Matosinhos (Porto metropolitan area)
- **CEO**: Rui Lopes Ferreira (CEO since 2015; previously CFO at Unicer 2006-2015, CFO at Vista Alegre Atlantis 2004-2006, 20 years at BPI, started at Coopers & Lybrand)
- **Chairman**: Manuel Violas (PCA)
- **Employees**: ~1,200-1,400 (varies by source; PitchBook 2026: 1,413)
- **Market position**: #1 beer brand in Portugal since 1992 (~47% group market share; ~30% Super Bock individual brand share)
- **Super Bock brand created**: 1927 (at Exposicao Industrial Portuguesa, Palacio de Cristal, Porto)
- **Awards**: 35+ gold medals at Monde Selection de la Qualite; WARC Effective 100 (2025) -- only Portuguese brand, #2 in Alcoholic Beverages globally
- **Not publicly listed** -- limited financial transparency

### Ownership Structure (Critical for Strategy)

```
Viacer (56%) -----> Super Bock Group <----- Carlsberg Group (44%)
  |
  |- Violas SGPS (71.5% of Viacer)
  |- Carlsberg Group (28.5% of Viacer)
```

**Effective Carlsberg control**: Carlsberg holds 44% directly + 28.5% of Viacer's 56% = total economic interest of **~60%**. Violas controls Viacer (71.5%) and therefore the 56% controlling stake, but Carlsberg is the de facto dominant economic stakeholder.

### The Carlsberg Paradox (Central Strategic Tension)

- Carlsberg owns ~60% economic interest but does not fully control strategy (Violas controls Viacer)
- Super Bock's internationalization may conflict with Carlsberg's own brand strategy
- Carlsberg benefits from domestic dominance but may not support international expansion competing with its own brands
- Somersby (cider) sold under Carlsberg license (~75% of PT cider market) -- dependency and risk if license terminated
- **Carlsberg Britvic acquisition** (Jan 2025, GBP 3.3B): Carlsberg shifts toward multi-beverage (soft drinks now 30% of portfolio); could increase interest in Super Bock's water brands (Vitalis, Pedras)
- Carlsberg FY 2024: Net revenue DKK 75.01B (+2.4% organic); Western Europe operating margin 13.9% (+60bps); targets debt/EBITDA below 2.5x by 2027; Post-Britvic synergy target GBP 100M by 2029

## Key Strategic Issues (for exam)

1. **The Carlsberg dilemma**: With ~60% economic interest, Carlsberg is the de facto owner but doesn't fully control strategy. Does Carlsberg constrain or enable growth? Would Super Bock be better off without Carlsberg (losing capital, know-how, Somersby license) or with them (accepting strategic constraints)?
2. **Internationalization ceiling**: At ~20% international, heavily domestic-dependent. Can it realistically grow to 40-50% given Carlsberg's competing brands?
3. **China concentration**: #1 export market (~40% of exports) is both opportunity (huge market, 5,000 points of sale) and risk (geopolitical, trade tensions, single-market dependency).
4. **Domestic market maturity**: 93% duopoly with Sagres leaves near-zero domestic volume growth. Is premiumization enough?
5. **Financial opacity**: Not disclosing results since 2018 limits benchmarking, talent attraction, and partnerships. Deliberate strategy or governance weakness?
6. **Non-alcoholic and health trends**: Super Bock 0.0 has proprietary technology advantage (Porto University dealcoholisation). How fast should they pivot?
7. **Craft beer defense**: 2-3% craft share is small but growing. Should SBG acquire craft brands or develop own?
8. **EUR 300M investment**: Major commitment for a company this size. Will it generate adequate returns? >EUR 100M already executed.
9. **Somersby license risk**: ~75% of Portuguese cider market under Carlsberg license. If Carlsberg exits JV or terminates license, SBG loses this overnight.
10. **Antitrust legacy**: 20-year price-fixing scheme (EUR 24M fine) raises governance questions; class action ongoing.
11. **The Central Question**: Super Bock's strategy is fundamentally constrained by the Carlsberg-Violas shareholder structure. Any analysis must address: **What would Carlsberg allow?**

## Supply Chain & Operations -- Leca do Balio Brewery (450M L Capacity), 33% Headroom

### Production Infrastructure

| Facility | Location | Function | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Leca do Balio brewery | Matosinhos (Porto metro) | Main brewery + HQ | 450M L capacity (2012 rebuild, 20-year design horizon); ~300M L current = ~33% spare capacity |
| Pedras Salgadas | Vila Real district | Sparkling water production | Capacity increased 2018 |
| Castelo de Vide | Portalegre district | Water bottling | Production center |
| Ladeira de Envendos | Santarem district | Water bottling | Active energy community |
| Santarem | Santarem | Production facility | Solar panels being installed |
| Maltiberica | Portugal | Malt production | 80% Portuguese dryland barley |
| Casa da Cerveja | Porto | Tourism/experiential | Brewery experience center |

### Raw Materials

| Input | Source | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Barley | 80% from dryland Portuguese fields | Maltiberica program supporting Portuguese farmers |
| Hops | Imported + domestic trials | Limited Portuguese cultivation |
| Water | Own springs (Vitalis, Pedras) | 2.88 L/L beverage (target 2.2) |
| Glass bottles | External suppliers | Lightweight bottles saved ~2,600 tonnes CO2 |
| Energy | 5% renewable (2024) | Target 98% by 2030; 1/3 electricity (solar), 2/3 thermal (biomass transition) |

### Supply Chain Risks

1. **Single brewery dependency**: Leca do Balio is primary beer facility -- disruption would be catastrophic
2. **Energy costs**: Thermal energy (2/3 of consumption) is largest cost driver after raw materials
3. **Barley price volatility**: Climate change affecting Portuguese dryland agriculture
4. **Glass supply**: Global shortages have affected beverage industry
5. **Import dependency for hops**: Minimal Portuguese hop cultivation
6. **Seasonal demand**: Summer/tourism season creates production peaks

## Technology & Innovation -- Proprietary Dealcoholisation, Super Bock Gold for China

### Non-Alcoholic Beer (Super Bock 0.0)

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Development partner | Porto University, Faculty of Engineering (Chemical Engineering Department) |
| Process | Full fermentation followed by dealcoholisation (not stopped fermentation) |
| Advantage | Preserves authentic beer taste vs. competitors using arrested fermentation |
| First Saudi export | August 2013, 500,000 liters |
| Export-only flavors | Lemon, Apple, Pomegranate, Strawberry, Dates |
| Somersby Pear 0.0 | First non-alcoholic cider produced in Portugal (2021) |

**Strategic value**: Proprietary dealcoholisation technology is a genuine competitive advantage. Most craft/regional brewers use simpler stopped-fermentation techniques with inferior taste. Aligns with quality positioning (35+ Monde Selection medals).

### Other Innovation

- Super Bock Gold: China-exclusive variant with different formula for Chinese palate
- No significant disclosed AI or digital transformation initiatives (contrast with AB InBev's "Beer Garage" innovation hub)

## Customer Base -- Off-Trade 55-60%, On-Trade 35-40%, Retail Power Dynamics

### Channel Mix

| Channel | Estimated Share | Key Players |
|---|---|---|
| Off-trade (retail/supermarket) | ~55-60% | Sonae (Continente), Jeronimo Martins (Pingo Doce), Auchan, Lidl |
| On-trade (bars, restaurants, hotels) | ~35-40% | Independent HoReCa, hotel chains |
| Export | ~20% | International distributors |

### Customer Dynamics

- **Retail power**: Sonae + Jeronimo Martins likely account for 30-40% of domestic off-trade
- **Duopoly protection**: Retailers need both Super Bock and Sagres -- consumer loyalty limits private label substitution
- **On-trade lock-in**: Exclusive pouring contracts create switching costs
- **Tourism seasonality**: 30-40% of on-trade revenue concentrated in June-September
- **Export concentration**: China at ~40% of exports creates single-market dependency
